A man speaks warmly, "Good evening, dear listeners, and welcome back to the After Hours Hour. I hope wherever you are tonight, you have a warm drink in your hand and a soft chair beneath you." He chuckles, "Hehe, because tonight, I have a story for you. A real one. The kind my grandfather used to tell me when the winter winds got too loud and the windows started to rattle." He sighs heavily, "It was the year nineteen forty-seven. My grandfather was a fisherman on the coast of Maine, and that November, the sea had gone strange." He speaks heavily, "Now, the old timers always said the ocean has moods, just like a person. Some days she is generous. Other days she is bored. And sometimes, once every twenty or thirty years, she gets restless." He pauses, "That November, she was restless." He continues, "Grandfather and his crew, three good men, went out at dawn on the fifteenth. The water was calm. Too calm. Like glass. And the gulls, there were no gulls. Not one. Just silence." He breathes deeply, "Grandfather said the engine on their little boat sounded twice as loud as usual, because there was nothing else to compete with it." He speaks heavily, "Have you ever heard true silence on the open ocean? It is a sound. A real, physical sound. It presses against your ears like cold water." He speaks evenly, "They were six miles out when the fog rolled in. Now, fog at sea is normal, but this came in fast. Like a curtain being drawn. One moment, they could see the coastline. The next, they could not see the bow of their own boat." He chuckles darkly, "And of course, the compass started spinning. Grandfather always said that is when he knew something was wrong, because the compass had never lied to him before, not in twenty years." He speaks heavily, "The wireless was dead. Pure static. So they did what fishermen do. They cut the engine, dropped anchor, and waited. Waited for the fog to lift. Waited for the sea to speak." He sighs heavily, "And eventually, she did. But not with words." He pauses, "First, they heard a bell. A church bell. Out there, in the middle of nowhere, six miles from shore." He chuckles darkly, "Heh, can you imagine? Three grown men, all fishermen, all of them religious in their own quiet way, sitting in a little wooden boat in a fog you could cut with a knife, listening to a church bell that had no business being there." He speaks heavily, "The bell came again. Closer. And then Grandfather always paused at this part. He said they heard singing. Soft. Distant. A woman's voice. In a language none of them recognized." He breathes deeply, "The youngest crewman, a boy named Thomas, just nineteen years old, started crying. Not sobbing, just tears running down his face, quiet, like he could not stop them." He speaks with quiet awe, "Then, ladies and gentlemen, came the strangest part. The fog around their boat, just around their boat, began to glow. A soft, pearl colored light. From below. From the water itself." He pauses, "Grandfather leaned over the side. And what he saw down there, he would never speak of clearly. Not to me. Not to anyone. He said only this, that the ocean was looking back at him. And it knew his name." He speaks warmly, "The fog lifted at sunrise, just like that. Like nothing had happened. The compass started working again. The wireless crackled back to life. And there they were, six miles from shore, in a calm sea, with a gentle breeze. As if nothing had happened." He speaks heavily, "Thomas, the young one, quit fishing the next week. Said he was going to seminary. He became a priest, lived to be ninety, and never once set foot on a boat again." He bursts into uncontrollable laughter, "Hahaha! And you know what Grandfather did? He kept fishing. For another forty years. Said the sea had given him permission, and he was not about to waste it." He speaks with quiet awe, "Now, I do not know what they saw out there that November. Grandfather never told me, and I never pressed him. But every time I see fog on the water, I think about that story. And I think about how big the world really is. How much of it we do not understand. How much we are not meant to." He gives a short throat clear, "Mmm. Anyway. That is my story for tonight, dear listeners. I hope it gave you something to think about while you sip your tea, or your whiskey, or whatever it is that gets you through the small hours." He chuckles, "Hahaha, no judgment from me, friend. We all have our ways." He speaks warmly, "I will be back tomorrow night, same time, with another one. Until then, take care of yourselves. Take care of the people around you. And if you ever find yourself on the open water in a fog you cannot see through, just listen. Listen carefully. Sometimes the sea has things to tell us. Good night, dear listeners. Sleep well."
